Background

Why CALI?

The Critical AI Literacy Institute

1
November 30, 2022 — ChatGPT drops, immediate effects on classrooms, university systems, and public discourse
2
Instructors at CUNY getting contradictory signals about what to do with these tools
3
“Critical literacy” = examining AI on terms that reflect disciplines, institution, and students
4
Not a stance against the technology — a commitment to examining it
Background

What is CALI?

Three-year initiative funded by Google.org, led by the TLC

1
Faculty development — technical sessions on system prompts, model parameters, APIs
2
Research — material/ecological costs, cognitive implications, labor effects
3
T(h)inkering — hands-on critical engagement through building
4
Advocacy — for the social conditions of teaching and learning at a public university
CALI Year One

Building Shared Vocabulary

Spring 2025 — technical foundations

1
Faculty wrote system prompts, manipulated parameters like temperature
2
Observed how a single setting changes what a language model produces
3
Built shared vocabulary around APIs, models, and parameters
4
Foundation for hands-on collaboration with faculty across disciplines

Transition

From CALI to CAIL

When the R&D outgrew the Institute

1
Faculty wanted a dedicated sandbox, stable chat platform, persistent tool infrastructure
2
Sustained technical support that a semester-long seminar could not provide
3
Proposal to Office of Academic Affairs, backed by GC Digital Initiatives, Mina Rees Library, American Social History Project
4
The CUNY AI Lab launched Spring 2026
The Lab

CUNY AI Lab

Mission

CUNY AI Lab

ailab.gc.cuny.edu

1
Faculty- and staff-led initiative at the GC for teaching, learning, and research across CUNY
2
Less about encouraging AI adoption — more about building critical literacies
3
Spotting the difference between AI snake oil and genuinely useful applications
4
Ambivalence is not a problem to solve — it’s a starting point
Platform

The Sandbox

chat.ailab.gc.cuny.edu

1
Open WebUI — free, open-source software we host and configure ourselves
2
Access to open-weight models: small local models to larger frontier models, single interface
3
Compare models side-by-side, write system prompts, adjust parameters, export transcripts
4
Share preset AI models like an open educational resource
5
Design decisions visible for collective scrutiny — unlike consumer platforms
Project

AmigAI

Heritage & non-native Spanish pedagogy

1
Sandbox supports five classes across CUNY, four involving AmigAI
2
Spanish instructors customize open-weight models for specific pedagogical needs
3
Surfacing problematic tendencies — e.g. flattening dialect-specific Spanish
4
Evaluative process identifies where guardrails are needed for responsible deployment
